The sky appears blue primarily due to the scattering of sunlight by Earth's atmosphere, a phenomenon known as Rayleigh scattering. Here's a detailed explanation:

1. **Sunlight Composition**: Sunlight consists of all the colors of the rainbow: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet. Each color corresponds to a specific wavelength, with red having the longest wavelength and violet the shortest.

2. **Rayleigh Scattering**: When sunlight enters Earth's atmosphere, it interacts with molecules and small particles in the air. Rayleigh scattering, named after Lord Rayleigh, explains that shorter wavelengths (blue and violet) are scattered more efficiently than longer wavelengths due to their smaller size relative to the wavelength of light.

3. **Blue Dominance**: Despite blue being less sensitive to human vision compared to violet, it is perceived as dominant because our eyes have cones sensitive to blue light, unlike other species. Additionally, the Sun emits more blue light towards the middle of its spectrum.

4. **Atmospheric Thickness**: At higher altitudes (sunrise and sunset), the atmosphere is thinner, so less scattering occurs, revealing longer wavelengths like red and orange as the sky appears those colors. Conversely, when the atmosphere is thicker, shorter wavelengths dominate, making the sky appear blue.

5. **Other Scattering Factors**: In hazy conditions, particles can scatter light differently, potentially altering the perceived color of the sky beyond what Rayleigh scattering alone explains.

In summary, the sky's blue appearance is a result of Rayleigh scattering scattering shorter blue and violet wavelengths more than longer ones, making them dominant in the clear sky. Our sensitivity to blue further enhances this perception.
